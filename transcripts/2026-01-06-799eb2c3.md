## Scale Intuition

For raw text search (find all occurrences of X), ripgrep is absurdly fast. Rough breakpoints:

| Size | Words | ripgrep | Dedicated search value |
|------|-------|---------|----------------------|
| <500KB | <100k | instant | none |
| 1-10MB | 100k-2M | <1 sec | marginal |
| 10-100MB | 2M-20M | noticeable | worthwhile |
| 100MB+ | 20M+ | slow | clear win |

Your 60k word manuscript is ~350KB. ripgrep won't break a sweat.

**But size isn't the only factor.** A search engine helps when you need:
- Fuzzy matching ("protagonsit" ‚Üí "protagonist")
- Relevance ranking (best matches first, not just all matches)
- Faceted filtering (show me scenes tagged "flashback" mentioning "mother")
- Typo tolerance for exploratory "what was that thing..." queries

For disciplined grep usage with known terms, these don't matter much. For exploratory "I vaguely remember writing something about..." searches, they're nice.

## Meilisearch vs Elasticsearch

**Both simpler operationally AND better UX for this task.**

| Aspect | Elasticsearch | Meilisearch |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Startup | Configure JVM, cluster settings, mappings | Single binary, zero config |
| RAM | Wants 2-4GB minimum | 50-100MB for small data |
| Indexing | Define mappings, analyzers | POST JSON, it figures it out |
| Typo tolerance | Manual analyzer configuration | On by default |
| Query syntax | Verbose JSON DSL | `?q=search+terms` |
| Target use | Distributed log analytics | Document search, dev UX |

ES was built for "search across terabytes in a cluster." Meilisearch was built for "make search work in 5 minutes."

**Bottom line:** We won't need either for this project, but if we did, meilisearch would be the obvious choice.

---

**What works**

The doubling is the engine. Farrell and Morrow as two iterations of the same broken Mexican War veteran‚Äîone trying to fill the emptiness with whiskey and work, the other embracing it and seeking a sacred death‚Äîgives the whole book its moral structure without ever becoming a thesis. You let Farrell recognize it explicitly ("We were two men formed from the same clay, fired in the same kiln") but only once, and late. That's correct.

The voices stay separate. Farrell is flat, declarative, doesn't elaborate. Morrow is more formal, almost homiletic‚Äî"I am being refined. I feel it happening." Neither slips. The intercalated structure creates dramatic irony (we know who killed the Hardins long before Farrell does) without feeling cheap. You're ahead of the investigator, but you're watching *him* catch up, which is its own kind of suspense.

The winter journey accumulates. The ice nearly breaking. The horse failing. The tremors in Farrell's hands, the sweat despite the cold, the visions crowding in as the whiskey runs out. By the time he finds the camp, he's as broken as the man he's been chasing. That convergence feels earned.

The restraint in the ending. No shootout. No confrontation. Farrell arrives too late, finds the body, sees the white buffalo, doesn't shoot. The Lakota take her north. Farrell collapses in the snow. The frame doesn't close‚ÄîAldridge never gets his report, the "another story" that follows is only gestured at. The sacred thing passes out of reach and the novel just stops. That's the right call. A more resolved ending would have betrayed the material.

---

**What gave me pause**

The visions. By Chapter 9, "the soldiers walked beside me" has appeared often enough that I started to glide past it. The DTs are doing real work‚Äîthey literalize the dead Farrell carries‚Äîbut the repetition risks turning them from haunting into furniture. I found myself wishing for one less invocation, to keep the effect unsettled.

Morrow's journal carries almost too much weight. It explains everything‚Äîhis history, his reasoning, the Hardin murders, the two years of waiting. By the time Farrell reads it in Chapter 10, we've already seen nearly all of it in the interleaved entries. The revelation scene becomes less a discovery than a confirmation. I wondered if a few more redactions‚Äîfragments where we infer rather than know‚Äîmight preserve some mystery.

The perspective shift in Chapter 12. After eleven chapters closely tied to Farrell's consciousness, you pull out to the Lakota riders and then to an omniscient view of the snow-covered valley. Beautifully written ("The snow continued to fall... It covered the dead man completely, erasing him from the world"), but jarring after the intimacy you've built. I wasn't sure if it was expansion or escape.

Aldridge. The opening chapter does careful work establishing him‚Äîhis grief, his uncertainty, the moral compromise of hiring someone to "deal with" the killer. But the frame never closes. He's still in Oswego, still waiting. That asymmetry might be intentional‚Äîthis is a story about the wilderness, not civilized accounting‚Äîbut the careful setup makes his absence from the ending feel like something left undone rather than something purposefully abandoned.

---

**Where it sits**

This isn't a genre Western. There's no law, no revenge, no climax in the expected sense. It's closer to the McCarthy of *Blood Meridian* without the baroque rhetoric, or to certain bleak literary Westerns that use the landscape as antagonist and the violence as interior condition. The prose is clean‚Äîit knows when not to elaborate, which is rarer than it sounds.

The title creature is more symbol than character, which is correct. Making her numinous in voice would have tipped the book into fantasy. Instead she just *is*: grazing, walking, killing when provoked. The meaning is projected onto her by the men who need her to mean something.

I'd read more from this writer. The patience here‚Äîboth in structure and sentence‚Äîsuggests someone thinking about what fiction can do rather than just doing it.
