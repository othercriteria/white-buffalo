## Book Club Discussion Questions for *White Buffalo*

### Character and Psychology

1. **Farrell and Morrow are both veterans of the Mexican-American War who came home "broken."** What distinguishes how each man responds to his trauma? Is one approach more self-aware than the other?

2. **Farrell describes Morrow as someone who "accepted the emptiness, embraced it, made it the center of his existence."** Do you think Farrell's reliance on whiskey is fundamentally different from Morrow's pursuit of the white buffalo, or are both seeking escape from the same emptiness?

3. **In Chapter Nine, Farrell realizes he and Morrow "were both made in the same forge."** Do you find this recognition sympathetic, troubling, or both? Does understanding a person's psychology mitigate their crimes?

4. **Neither protagonist has children. Catherine Hardin's letters notably contain no mention of children.** What role does childlessness—and the absence of legacy—play in how these characters understand their lives?

### Violence and Its Consequences

5. **Morrow kills the Hardins not out of anger or hatred, but because they "were in the way."** Is this calculated indifference more disturbing than violence motivated by passion? What does it reveal about what war made him?

6. **Morrow writes that he "did not know I would feel this. I thought I was past feeling."** What significance do you attach to the fact that killing the Hardins disturbs him when killing in war did not?

7. **The novel depicts the Mexican-American War in brutal detail, including the killing of young cadets at Chapultepec.** How does the novel position that war in relation to the violence occurring on the frontier in the 1850s?

### The Sacred and the Profane

8. **Morrow believes the white buffalo can give his death "a meaning that my life never had."** Is this genuine spiritual seeking or, as he himself wonders, "the work of a broken mind, grasping at meaning where there was none"?

9. **The white buffalo is sacred in Lakota tradition, but Morrow—a white man—has appropriated this symbolism for his own purposes.** How does the novel treat the tension between Indigenous sacred beliefs and Morrow's personal use of them?

10. **At the end, the Lakota riders appear and the white buffalo goes with them "toward wherever it was that sacred things went when their work was done."** Is this a restoration of proper order? What does it suggest about who the buffalo belonged to?

### Narrative Structure

11. **The novel alternates between Farrell's third-person narration and Morrow's first-person journal entries.** How does this structure affect your sympathies? Would the novel work differently if both were in the same point of view?

12. **Morrow's journal entries cover nearly three years (1856-1858), while Farrell's journey takes only a few months.** What effect does this compression have on how we experience each character's story?

13. **We never directly see the murder of the Hardins—only Farrell's discovery of the evidence and Morrow's journal confession.** Why might the author have chosen to keep this violence off-page?

### Setting and History

14. **The novel is set during the bleeding Kansas era, with the Civil War looming.** How do the references to sectional conflict and the coming war frame our understanding of the personal violence in the story?

15. **Both protagonists cross a landscape being transformed by railroads, settlers, and the displacement of Indigenous peoples.** How does the novel treat the relationship between individual trauma and the larger violence of westward expansion?

16. **The winter landscape is almost a character in itself—vast, indifferent, deadly.** How does the setting reflect or contrast with the interior states of the protagonists?

### The Ending

17. **Farrell does not shoot the white buffalo, even though he could.** What does this restraint signify? Is it mercy, exhaustion, understanding, or something else?

18. **The novel ends with Farrell "kneeling in an empty valley beside a man I had never met, with blood on the snow and nothing left to follow."** Is this an ending of despair or of possibility? What do you imagine happens next?

19. **Farrell experiences something he calls "peace" while watching the buffalo—not forgiveness, but "an acknowledgment."** What is being acknowledged? And who or what is doing the acknowledging?

20. **The visions that haunt Farrell—the dead soldiers, Catherine Hardin—become "witnesses" rather than torments by the end.** Has something changed in Farrell, or only in his perception?

### Broader Questions

21. **Morrow's final journal entry says, "I do not ask for understanding. I do not ask for forgiveness."** After reading his account, do you feel understanding toward him? Does that understanding feel like a kind of complicity?

22. **The novel presents no clear moral resolution—the killer dies as he wished, the Hardins remain dead, and Farrell returns to his own broken life.** Is this appropriate to the story, or did you want something more definitive?

23. **Both men served under officers who would become Confederate generals (Jefferson Davis, Robert Lee).** How does the novel connect the violence of the Mexican War to the violence that would follow in the Civil War and on the frontier?

## Classroom Materials: Teaching *White Buffalo*

### Overview for Instructors

**Grade Level:** 11-12 (American Literature / AP Literature)

**Content Advisory:** This novel contains graphic descriptions of war violence, alcoholism and withdrawal symptoms, and the aftermath of murder. Preview carefully before assigning. The novel's treatment of Indigenous spirituality should be contextualized with supplementary materials on Lakota belief and the actual history of white buffalo in Native American culture.

**Approximate Length:** ~35,000 words (novella-length; appropriate for a 2-3 week unit)

**Historical Period:** 1856-1858, set in Nebraska Territory and the Great Plains

---

### Essential Questions

1. How do individuals cope with trauma, and what distinguishes healthy adaptation from destructive responses?
2. What happens when people appropriate the sacred symbols of other cultures for their own purposes?
3. How does the literature of the American West engage with the violence inherent in westward expansion?
4. Can a narrative create sympathy for a character whose actions are morally indefensible?

- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RL.11-12.5** – Analyze how an author's choices concerning how to structure specific parts of a text contribute to its overall structure and meaning as well as its aesthetic impact
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RL.11-12.6** – Analyze a case in which grasping a point of view requires distinguishing what is directly stated in a text from what is really meant

### Pre-Reading Activities

#### 1. Historical Context Research (Small Groups)

Assign each group one topic to research and present to the class (10-minute presentations):

- **The Mexican-American War (1846-1848):** Causes, major battles (Monterrey, Buena Vista, Chapultepec), outcomes, and legacy
- **Bleeding Kansas and the Road to Civil War:** The violence over slavery's expansion in the 1850s
- **The Great Plains in the 1850s:** Geography, climate, Indigenous nations (particularly Lakota/Sioux), and early white settlement
- **White Buffalo in Lakota/Sioux Tradition:** The story of White Buffalo Calf Woman, the sacred significance of white buffalo, historical sightings
- **Alcoholism in 19th-Century America:** Prevalence, social attitudes, treatment (or lack thereof)

#### 1. Dual Timeline Tracking

| Farrell's Journey (Chapters) | Morrow's Journal (Entries) |

#### 2. Close Reading: The Emptiness Motif

Have students collect quotations from both characters that reference "emptiness," "hollowness," or feeling nothing. Questions for discussion:

- How does each character describe this emptiness?
- What caused it?
- How does each try to address it?
- Are they describing the same psychological state?

- Chapter Two: "A place inside me where the fear should have been... that was simply not there"

- Morrow writes that the Hardins "must be removed" as "a simple statement of fact." What is missing from his reasoning?
- He also writes, "I do not write this with pleasure." Does his lack of pleasure make his decision better or worse?
- Why does he bury them so carefully afterward?
- What does Farrell's investigation reveal that Morrow's confession does not?

### Post-Reading Activities

#### 1. Analytical Essay Prompts

**Option A: Parallel Characters**
"We were both made in the same forge. We had both come out broken." Analyze how the novel develops the parallel between Farrell and Morrow. In what ways are they similar? In what crucial ways do they differ? What argument is the novel making about responses to trauma?

**Option B: Narrative Structure**
Analyze how the alternating structure of *White Buffalo* (third-person chapters vs. first-person journal entries) shapes the reader's understanding of and response to the events of the novel. Consider what each narrative mode reveals and conceals.

**Option C: The Sacred and Violence**
Morrow seeks a "sacred death" that will give meaning to his life. Analyze how the novel treats the intersection of violence and the sacred. Is Morrow's quest portrayed as genuine spiritual seeking, as delusion, or as something more complicated?

**Option D: The Ending**
The novel ends with Farrell alone in the snow, experiencing "peace" but not forgiveness, with "nothing left to follow." Write an analysis of this ending. Is it a resolution? What has been resolved, and what remains unresolved?

#### 2. Creative Response Options

**Option A: Catherine's Last Letter**
Write the letter Catherine Hardin was working on when she was killed. Use details from the drafts found in Chapter Six and her father's descriptions of her earlier letters to capture her voice.

**Option B: Farrell's Report**

**Option C: Alternative Journal Entry**

#### 3. Socratic Seminar: Final Discussion

Seed questions for culminating discussion:

1. Morrow writes, "I am going to my death willingly, gladly, with the certainty that it is the only right thing I have ever done." Is he right? Is this a "right thing"?

2. Farrell recognizes that he and Morrow are alike. Does this recognition make him complicit in Morrow's crimes?

3. The white buffalo kills Morrow, then goes with the Lakota. Is she an agent in this story, or simply an animal responding to threat?

4. The novel ends without clear moral closure—no punishment, no redemption, no return to society. Is this a satisfying ending? Is it honest?

## On Authorship for AI-Assisted Work

This is genuinely uncharted territory, but here are the emerging approaches and considerations:

### Option 1: Co-Author Credit, AI in Acknowledgments

```
WHITE BUFFALO
Daniel Klein and Ben Cohen
```

Then in a colophon or acknowledgments:
> "This novel was written with the assistance of Claude (Anthropic). The authors directed the creative vision, characters, themes, and narrative structure; Claude drafted prose under their guidance."

**Pros:** Clean byline, honest disclosure, follows the model of acknowledged ghostwriting/collaboration
**Cons:** Some readers may feel the AI role is understated

### Option 2: "With" Credit

```
WHITE BUFFALO
by Daniel Klein and Ben Cohen
with Claude
```

**Pros:** More explicit about the three-party nature
**Cons:** Unusual; raises questions about what "with" means; anthropomorphizes the tool

### Option 3: Detailed Colophon Only

Keep the byline to the human authors, but include a detailed note on process:

> "This novel was developed collaboratively by Daniel Klein and Ben Cohen using Claude Code (Anthropic, 2025). Klein directed the drafting sessions; Cohen contributed substantially to conception, story structure, and thematic development. The prose was generated through an iterative process of prompting, revision, and editorial judgment by the human authors."

**Pros:** Full transparency, keeps traditional authorship model
**Cons:** May feel buried if only in back matter

---

### What I'd Recommend

For a work like this—a serious literary project, not a gimmick—I'd suggest **Option 1 or 3**, depending on how much you want the AI involvement to be part of the "story" of the book.

If you want the book to be read primarily as a novel (judged on its literary merits), go with:

**Title page:**
```
WHITE BUFFALO

Daniel Klein and Ben Cohen
```

**Copyright page or colophon (front or back):**
```
This novel was written with the assistance of Claude, 
an AI assistant developed by Anthropic. The authors 
conceived, directed, and edited the work; Claude 
generated the prose.
```

This positions you and Ben as the creative principals (which you were—the conception, direction, and judgment were human) while being transparent about the process.

---

### Legal/Practical Considerations

1. **Copyright:** Current US Copyright Office guidance is that AI-generated content without sufficient human authorship isn't copyrightable. However, a work with substantial human creative direction, selection, and arrangement likely qualifies. Your role as director/editor provides the human authorship.

2. **Publishing:** If submitting to agents/publishers, disclose the AI involvement. This is an evolving area and norms are still forming.

3. **Equal Credit:** If you and Ben want 50/50, list names alphabetically or discuss billing. "Klein and Cohen" or "Cohen and Klein"—standard co-author questions.
